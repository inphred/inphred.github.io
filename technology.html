The core insight
GaAs VCSELs dominate NIR because AlGaAs/GaAs delivers ~16% refractive-index contrast — enough for >99.9% mirror reflectivity. InP and GaN lattice-matched alloys offer only ~0.3%. InPHRED's nanoporous etching engineers that contrast from scratch — on the same foundry epi the industry already uses.
The breakthrough

30-year deadlock — and how we solved it

The industry has been stuck for 30 years
1
GaAs VCSELs dominate NIR (850–980 nm) because AlGaAs/GaAs forms a near-perfect homoepitaxial DBR mirror pair — ~16% refractive-index contrast, mature process, wafer-level testable, low cost. This is why VCSELs are in every iPhone and LiDAR sensor today.
2
InP (SWIR) and GaN (visible) have no equivalent DBR pair. Lattice-matched InP alloys yield only ~0.3% index contrast versus ~16% for AlGaAs/GaAs — far below the ~99.9% mirror reflectivity required to sustain a VCSEL optical cavity. GaN faces the same fundamental barrier.
3
All workarounds fail to scale. Heteroepitaxial growth is expensive and defect-prone. Wafer fusion disrupts the monolithic process flow. Double dielectric DBRs require complex post-processing. No competitor has achieved low-cost, wafer-level InP or GaN VCSELs commercially.
InPHRED's patented nanoporous solution
✓
Electrochemical nanoporous etching selectively converts n⁺-doped semiconductor layers into nanoporous material — reducing local refractive index by a precisely controllable amount, determined entirely by porosity. Undoped layers remain unchanged.
✓
Homoepitaxial DBR with engineered index contrast. Alternating dense and nanoporous layers create refractive-index steps of up to ~40% — matching or exceeding AlGaAs/GaAs — on standard foundry-grown InP or GaN epi. No exotic materials, no wafer fusion, no dielectric layers.
✓
Drop-in compatible with volume manufacturing. The porosification step is a single post-epi electrochemical process added to a conventional wafer-level flow. It uses existing foundry equipment and enables on-wafer testing — no disruption to the compound semiconductor supply chain.
Mechanism

How nanoporous DBR mirrors are made

The process starts with structures the industry already knows how to grow — and adds one precisely controlled post-epi step.

Step 01

Start with foundry epi

Begin with mature telecom InP (or GaN) epitaxial structures featuring alternating n⁺-doped and undoped layers. No custom growth recipe required — existing 1,550 nm telecom epi is a direct starting point.

Step 02

Selective electrochemical anodisation

An electrochemical etch selectively converts only the n⁺-doped layers to nanoporous semiconductor. The undoped layers are impervious to etching — creating a precisely defined alternating stack of dense and porous material.

Step 03

Tunable refractive-index profile

Porosity — controlled by current density, etch time, and electrolyte composition — sets the local refractive index of each porous layer. Index contrast up to ~40% is achievable, far exceeding what InP alloys can provide.

Step 04

High-reflectivity DBR mirror stack

The alternating dense/porous layer pair acts as a Bragg reflector. With sufficient pairs and contrast, reflectivity exceeds 99.5% — the threshold required for VCSEL lasing. This was previously unachievable homoepitaxially in InP or GaN.

Step 05

Conventional wafer-level VCSEL / RC-LED fabrication

The remainder of the process follows a standard semiconductor flow: mesa etch, contact metallisation, and packaging — all compatible with existing compound semiconductor foundries. On-wafer testing is fully supported.

Step 06

Volume production at low cost

Because the DBR is homoepitaxial and the process is wafer-level, yield is high and cost is low — comparable to GaAs VCSEL economics, not the costly alternatives (wafer fusion, dielectric DBRs) that competitors have attempted.
